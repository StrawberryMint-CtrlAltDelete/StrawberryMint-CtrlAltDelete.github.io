Brief Description

Dementia is the broad term used to describe a number of different conditions affecting the brain
its affect can be is physical, emotional and psychological,
it can also profoundly change the practicalities of everyday life
Dementia is not only about memory loss. It can also affect the way you speak, think, feel and behave.


the most common types of dementia are:



Alzheimer’s disease - most common type (60% of diagnosis)
Vascular dementia - second most common in older people
Frontotemporal dementia - common in younger people
Lewy body dementia - affects movement and motor control
and others - some of which can be genetic ushc as huntington's




Dementia may cause problems with:


memory

mental sharpness and quickness
language, such as using words incorrectly, or trouble speaking
understanding
judgement
mood
movement


as well as;


difficulties doing daily activities
problems managing their behaviour or emotions.
dificulty with relationships and socialising.
see or hear things that other people do not - hallucinations

Demetia Care


there is currenty no cure for dementia;
however there are ways to manage symptoms and minimise the impact

Some of these treatments are;


Person-centred care;


The Aim of Person-centred care to insure people with dementia can do the things they enjoy
It can be an effective way of preventing and managing the psychological symptoms of dementia.

The key points of person centred care are;

Treating the person with dignity and respect
understanding their history, lifestyle, culture and preferences, including their likes, dislikes, hobbies and interests
looking at situations from the point of view of the person with dementia
providing opportunities for the person to have conversations and relationships with other people
ensuring the person has the chance to try new things or take part in activities they enjoy.

Family, carers and the person with dementia
should always be involved in developing a care plan based on person-centred care.

As Person-centred care is deeply personal;
knowledge and understanding of the person is invaluable when developing Person-centred care


Psychological(talking) Therapy

Talking therapies can be effective for people in the early to middle stages of dementia.
In psychological therapy a person will be supported to talk about how their thoughts and feelings affect their mood and behaviour.
Therapy sessions are delivered by a professional, such as a counsellor, a clinical or counselling psychologist, a psychotherapist, or a psychiatrist.
People can have some types of therapy over the phone or online.

Talking therapies are unlikely to help if a person is in the later stages


There are many types of therapy the most common for people with dementia are:

Counselling
Psychotherapy
Cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT)

Although there are lots of others
How to find a therapist

Many GPs can give people details about therapists in the local area.
Some GP surgeries also have therapy services based in their practices.
Many talking therapies mentioned in this section are available through an NHS England programme called Improving Access to Psychological Therapies (IAPT)
